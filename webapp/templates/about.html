overview

The ability to accurately detect orthologous or functionally equivalent proteins in different organisms is important to numerous biological research questions, including studies of variables influencing rate protein evolution [1-3], accurate genome annotation [4], and studies of proteins implicated in cancer [5]. Roundup was created to speed such research projects by making available an up-to-date set of orthologs for many genomes. To that end, Roundup is "released" 4 times a year. For each release, the latest completed genomes are downloaded (see sources below), orthologs are computed for those genomes, and then made available on the website. For scientific reproducibility, previous releases are archived and available for download.

Roundup is an online database of gene orthologs for {{ numGenomes }} genomes computed using the reciprocal smallest distance (RSD) algorithm. Users may query Roundup for single-linkage clusters of orthologous genes based on any group of genomes. Annotated query results may be viewed in a variety of ways including as clusters of orthologs and as phylogenetic profiles. Genomic results may be downloaded in formats suitable for functional as well as phylogenetic analysis, including the recent OrthoXML standard. In addition, gene IDs can be retrieved using FASTA sequence search. All orthology results and source code are freely available.

Recovering the true set of orthologs between two lineages depend on many parameters, including date of divergence between the lineages, rates of gene duplication in either lineage, intensity of molecular selection, and others. To account for such variables that can have a large impact on the size and content of a list of orthologs, and to provide users of Roundup a certain degree of exploratory power, we adjusted two parameters -- BLAST E-value and global pair-wise sequence divergence -- when calculating orthology between genomes. Specifically, in our calculations we used four increasingly stringent BLAST scores, 1e-5, 1e-10, 1e-15, and 1e-20, and three increasingly stringent divergence thresholds, 0.8, 0.5, and 0.2. Therefore, for every pair of genomes, Roundup contains twelve ortholog lists representing all possible combinations of the two parameters.

algorithm

The reciprocal smallest distance (RSD) algorithm [1] represents an improvement over ortholog detection approaches that rely on blast hits alone, since it uses global rather than local sequence alignments and evolutionary estimates of distance between sequences rather than blast probability scores, an approach that can often be misleading when trying to determine functional equivalence [2].

RSD first uses BLAST [5] to net a list of possible orthologs between two genomes. This list is then filtered by E-value and a global alignment threshold (a.k.a. divergence) before evolutionary distance is estimated. Explicitly, if the alignable region exceeds a predefined fraction of the total length of the protein, a distance is calculated as a maximum likelihood estimate of the number of amino acid substitutions separating the two putatively orthologous protein sequences, given an empirical amino acid substitution rate matrix [6]. The smallest distance is considered to be consistent with a hypothesis of functional equivalency between the two proteins.

A paper describing the algorithm in more detail is avialable at Bioinformatics. The algorithm can be downloaded here.

overview

The ability to accurately detect orthologous or functionally equivalent proteins in different organisms is important to numerous biological research questions, including studies of variables influencing rate protein evolution [1-3], accurate genome annotation [4], and studies of proteins implicated in cancer [5]. The ability to retrieve orthologs from many genomes at the click of a button would help speed such research projects along tremendously.

To accelerate research this, we have computed orthologs for {{ numGenomes }} genomes using the reciprocal smallest distance (RSD) algorithm [6]. This method represents an improvement over approaches that rely on blast hits alone, since it uses global rather than local sequence alignments and evolutionary estimates of distance between sequences rather than blast probability scores, an approach that can often be misleading when trying to determine functional equivalence [7]. These computed sets of orthologs are stored in the publicly accessible database, Roundup, one of the most comprehensive of its kind [8, 9].

The reciprocal smallest distance algorithm first uses BLAST [10] to net a list of several possible orthologs between two genomes and then filters this list using a global alignment threshold and evolutionary distance measure. Explicitly, if the alignable region exceeds a predefined fraction of the total length of the protein, a distance is calculated as a maximum likelihood estimate of the number of amino acid substitutions separating the two putatively orthologous protein sequences, given an empirical amino acid substitution rate matrix [11]. The smallest distance is considered to be consistent with a hypothesis of functional equivalency between the two proteins.

Recovering the true set of orthologs between two lineages will depend on many parameters, including date of divergence between the lineages, rates of gene duplication in either lineage, intensity of molecular selection, and others. To account for such variables that can have a large impact on the size and content of a list of orthologs, and to provide users of Roundup a certain degree of exploratory power, we adjusted two parameters – BLAST E-value and global pair-wise sequence divergence – when calculating orthology between genomes. Specifically, in our calculations we used four increasingly stringent BLAST scores, 1e-5, 1e-10, 1e-15, and 1e-20, and three increasingly stringent divergence thresholds, 0.8, 0.5, and 0.2. Therefore, for every pair of genomes, our Roundup repository contains twelve ortholog lists representing all possible combinations of the two variables.

Presently, Roundup contains orthologs from all possible pair-wise analyses of {{ numGenomes }} genomes. The results are stored in relational form and accessible through a flexible web interface that allows the user to build arbitrarily complex and exploratory queries without needing to know how to construct SQL statements. For example, a user of Roundup can run queries to find all orthologs in all {{ numGenomes }} genomes for a given search sequence that are evolutionarily conserved, according to some a priori chosen distance threshold. Alternatively, a user may quickly build clusters of orthologous genes for any number of genomes, i.e., build groups of orthologs that exhibit complete transitive closure. We expect it will be of value to many molecular biological fields.
